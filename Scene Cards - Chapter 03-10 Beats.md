# Scene Cards — Chapter 3-10 Beats

## Ch 3 — The Altar Not Yet Built (1687-1689)

POV: Sukjong. ~27-28 years old. Seven years since the one word.

1. **The Chinese text arrives.** The Wanli/Jianwen restoration record reaches Sukjong's private study. Uncle seized throne from nephew. Two hundred years of silence. A later emperor restored the name. The parallel is exact.
2. **The parallel, in dialogue.** Sukjong discusses with a trusted scholar. Key insight: Wanli did not condemn the usurper. He acknowledged the victim was also legitimate. Both could be real. The shrine gets larger, not smaller.
3. **Nam returns.** Sukjong presents the formula. Nam recognizes the precedent changes the structural problem. But the political weather is shifting — the Southerners are regaining influence. Song Si-yeol's position weakening.
4. **The debt to Wanli.** Joseon owes the Wanli Emperor for the Imjin War. If Wanli restored his wronged nephew, Joseon's failure to do the same is not just internal — it dishonors the precedent set by the dynasty that saved them.
5. **The warning signs.** The factional balance is tilting. Jang Hui-bin's influence rising. Concrete scene showing the political pressure.
6. **The decision not to act.** Inverse of Ch 1. He has the full formula, chooses not to use it. Acting at the wrong time would discredit the formula along with the faction. Files the argument.
7. **The private commitment.** Sukjong writes the Wanli parallel in his own hand. Places it in private papers. The ambiguity: the commitment is sincere, and the future justification is strategic.

**Ending image:** The formula exists. The timing does not. A king filing away an argument he cannot yet speak.

## Ch 4 — The Queen's Fall (1689)

POV: Sukjong primary, Lady Yoon secondary. The Gisa Hwanguk.

1. **Sukjong's decision.** The crown prince question. Jang Hui-bin's son. The Westerners oppose. Sukjong proceeds — not about the concubine but about whether the court or the king decides succession.
2. **The purge begins.** Same machinery as Ch 1, pointed the other direction. Westerners stripped from office. The reader knows what's in the blast radius: the husband, his network, the formula.
3. **Song Si-yeol's execution.** The patriarch. The man who walked away from the fourteen-year-old king. The man who taught the husband. What it costs Sukjong — or doesn't.
4. **Lady Yoon's kitchen.** POV shifts. She is home. Husband gone on provincial trip. A visitor arrives with news — names mentioned, scholars questioned, temperature changed.
5. **The husband comes home.** Different. Tension in his jaw sharper. Goes to the study. Papers being rearranged. Dialogue: "Song Si-yeol is dead." / "What does that mean for us?" / "The people looking for his students will now look harder."
6. **The letters stop.** Correspondence thins. Contacts go silent. The husband writes fewer letters. Burns some. She watches from the corridor.
7. **Sukjong alone.** The purge complete. The formula useless — the faction that would carry it is gone. But somewhere in the provinces, a scholar who was documenting burial sites is now visible.
8. **The last letter.** A letter slips through the collapsing network. A warning connecting the documentation work to someone in the new government who wants it stopped. She reads it. Does not show it to her husband. Puts it in the desk compartment.

**Ending image:** Two rooms, two people. Sukjong holding a formula he cannot use. Lady Yoon holding a letter her husband does not know she has read.

## Ch 5 — The Murder (~1690-1691)

POV: Lady Yoon. Hwanguk fallout.

1. **The last ordinary morning.** The husband leaves for the provinces. Daily rhythm. She hands him the travel coat. A brief exchange — the friction that is their form of intimacy. He leaves.
2. **The silence begins.** He doesn't return on the expected day. Second day. Third. She sends a message through the network. The answer is a change in tone.
3. **The news arrives.** Someone comes. Careful language: illness on the road, sudden decline, body already handled. The account is smooth and closed.
4. **What the body tells her.** She goes to receive the body. Details that don't match the story. Wrong marks. Wrong timeline. Wrong geography.
5. **The witnesses go quiet.** Provincial contacts suddenly unavailable. Not hostile. Absent. Someone has been through the network before her.
6. **Master Kim.** He is old, ill. He comes anyway. Dialogue: he knows what happened. Cannot say all of it. Gives her what he can — papers, a name, a direction pointing beyond the province toward the capital.
7. **The transfer.** Master Kim gives her the archive. The network, the correspondence, the records. Not indirect placement this time. He looks at her and says it plainly, because there is no more time for the seowon's patient method.
8. **The fragment.** Among the papers — a document that connects the husband's death to a specific political actor in the capital. She holds the first piece.
9. **The doorway reverses.** The study is empty. The desk is hers. The work is hers. The men who kept her on the other side of the door are gone.

**Ending image:** The fragment in her hand. The empty study. The work ahead.

## Ch 6 — The Correspondence Web (~1691-1693)

POV: Lady Yoon.

1. **The problem of voice.** Letters arrive addressed to a dead man. She reads them. She has the network. She does not have the authority. She decides to answer.
2. **The first forgery.** She writes back in Master Kim's voice, in his hand. The register, the method of embedding questions — she has internalized it.
3. **The intermediary problem.** She needs someone to carry letters. (Nephew issue flagged — needs development. For now: she finds a male intermediary through the network.)
4. **The stone rubbing.** Among the husband's papers — a hand-copied impression of Eom Heung-do's memorial tablet. Supposedly destroyed decades ago. The rubbing proves it still existed. She sends an inquiry. Answer: destroyed. The rubbing says otherwise.
5. **The two lines converge.** Two threads of information arrive. One about the husband's movements before death. One about a name in the capital connected to the anti-restoration faction. Both threads point to the same person.
6. **Ok-sun returns.** Dialogue scene. Ok-sun has heard things — someone is asking questions about provincial scholars who documented burial sites. Official questions. Ok-sun is frightened.
7. **The letter that should not exist.** A misdirected reply containing the husband's name, connected to a specific political decision. A seal placing it inside the capital's apparatus. He was not killed by local politics. He was killed because his work reached someone with the power to stop it.
8. **The cost of the method.** A physical beat — her hand cramps, ink on her fingers, the same stain since she was a maid reading by firelight. The work continues. The cost is visible.

**Ending image:** Ink drying on a letter written in a dead man's voice. Two lines of inquiry meeting at a name she cannot yet speak aloud.

## Ch 7 — The Queen Restored (1694)

POV: Sukjong.

1. **The trap exposed.** Noron young men arrested for plotting to restore Queen Inhyeon — but it's a Southerner fabrication. Sukjong reads the evidence.
2. **The second reversal.** Southerners fall. Westerners return — but divided. Noron want Jang Hui-bin destroyed. Soron counsel moderation.
3. **Nam Guman, again.** Now Prime Minister. Mediates the queen crisis. Dialogue: "The decree has been issued. Arguing further is improper." He prevents a bloodbath. Sukjong watches.
4. **Inhyeon returns.** The queen restored. Not celebration — machinery. The position returns. The five years do not.
5. **The wreckage that remains.** Sukjong takes stock. The Westerners are back but damaged. Song Si-yeol dead. Networks disrupted. Men lost.
6. **The formula, revisited.** Sukjong retrieves the Wanli parallel from private papers. The formula works. But the court's trust has been broken by two reversals.
7. **The cost he cannot see.** He begins moving toward restoration. Somewhere in the provinces, the cost of his 1689 decision is alive in a widow running a dead man's network. The reader knows.

**Ending image:** Order returning over wreckage that remains where it fell.

## Ch 8 — The Inspector (~1696-1697)

POV: Yi Hwon.

1. **The garrison.** Yi Hwon in his military posting. Supply discrepancy in the records. He reports it. Nothing happens.
2. **The crossing.** Brief encounter with Lady Yoon — same space, neither registers the other. Three sentences. The reader carries it alone.
3. **The exam.** He sits for the munkwa. A military officer's son in the civil examination hall. Passes solidly, not brilliantly.
4. **Soonyi.** A scene before he leaves. The life that makes sense. Warmth, comfort, the expectation of return. She knows him. She does not challenge him. The scene feels like home — warm, safe, slightly small.
5. **The selection.** Sukjong's circle selects him as amhaeng-eosa. A report he filed from the garrison — the one that "disappeared" — reached someone who recognized it: an official who describes what he sees instead of what the system wants.
6. **The briefing.** The sealed credential. Travel to the southern provinces. Official mission: assess governance. Second layer: the political ground around Yeongwol. The restoration is coming.
7. **The road begins.** Alone, disguised. The country after two hwanguks. Provincial officials appointed and removed and reappointed. Policy after it has become weather.
8. **The first discovery.** An incident that makes the mission personal. The system about to correct a historical wrong is also, locally, still punishing people for caring about that wrong.

**Ending image:** A disguised inspector on a provincial road, carrying a sealed credential and walking toward a county where a woman he does not remember is running a network he does not know exists.

## Ch 9 — The Memorial (~1697)

POV: Yi Hwon and Lady Yoon.

1. **The ceremony.** A provincial memorial for Song Si-yeol. Scholars, former students, families. Yi Hwon is there because a higher-up sent him — he was passing through on his way back to the capital from his military posting, and someone in the office wanted eyes at the memorial. Watch who attends, read the temperature, report back. Routine assignment. Lady Yoon is there as widow of Song Si-yeol's student.
2. **The wealthy woman.** Lady Yoon navigates a social complication — a donor's wife who needs managing. She does it from the margins, controlling without being seen. Yi Hwon watches from across the courtyard. He sees first that she is a widow — the white clothes, the simple hair, the plainness that stands out in a room full of color. Then he sees how she moves. The contrast between the plain exterior and the social precision is what catches him.
3. **She notices him.** She observes everyone — it's what she does, the way she has done it since the seowon, since the corridor, since the first tray she carried past a room full of men who did not see her seeing them. She classifies: scholar, merchant, local official, visiting student. The room sorts itself. This one does not sort. He is dressed as a merchant, but his hands are wrong — calluses in the places where a man grips something heavier than a writing brush and lighter than a plow. His posture is wrong — weight centered, not shifted forward the way men who carry goods stand. He watches doorways, not buyers. She catalogues further: the shoulders, the jaw when he turns to listen to a conversation he is not part of, the calibrated attention of a man trained to assess a space before he occupies it. She is still cataloguing when he turns and looks directly at her. No surprise in it. No adjustment. He has been watching her classify him and is now waiting to see what she does next. She looks away — the smooth social redirect of a woman who has never needed it before. The ceremony ends. She tells the host she will return tomorrow. As she turns to leave, he is nearby — retying the cord on a merchant's bundle, close enough to have overheard. This man is either careless with his proximity or careful with it, and she cannot determine which.
4. **She's gone.** Something pulls her away overnight. Network business. She doesn't come back.
5. **The empty space.** Yi Hwon returns the next day. She is not there. He asks casually. The widow left. No name. No reason. The absence stays. A woman in white who managed a room and then vanished.
6. **The road continues.** He moves on with his investigation. But the image stays — filed like a discrepancy in supply records. Something that doesn't fit, noted, unresolved. A year will pass before he sees her face again. The white clothes are how he will recognize her before he sees it.

**Ending image:** A young inspector on the road south, carrying a sealed credential and the face of a woman he cannot name.
