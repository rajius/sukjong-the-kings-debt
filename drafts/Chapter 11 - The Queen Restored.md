# Chapter 11 — The Queen Restored

Five years after the Hwanguk, the evidence arrived before dawn on a gray morning in autumn.

Sukjong read the documents in his private study while Nam Guman waited in silence. The papers were convincing: correspondence between young Noron scholars, meeting schedules, fragments of plans to restore Queen Inhyeon through coordinated pressure on the throne. The kind of factional conspiracy that had justified the previous purge.

Except the handwriting was wrong.

"These letters," Sukjong said, setting down the evidence. "Who intercepted them?"

"Southerner agents. Minister Mok's network." Nam's expression was carefully neutral. "They claim to have discovered the plot through routine surveillance of Westerner activities."

Sukjong studied the documents again. The language was too crude, the political arguments too obvious. Real conspirators did not write letters that read like accusations against themselves.

"This is fabrication."

"Yes, Majesty."

Sukjong set the documents on the desk, face down. He said nothing for a long time.

"The fabricators have given me a gift," he said. "They intended it as a weapon. Make it a procedural matter."

Nam studied the king's face. Then he understood.

"Administrative fraud. The investigative process was compromised. The crown corrects the process, not the politics."

Sukjong waited.

"The Westerners return as part of restoring administrative balance. Not a reward. A correction." Nam paused. "And the investigation into the fabrication — conducted jointly. Ministers from both factions."

"Who cannot oppose it," Sukjong said, "without endorsing fraud."

Nam bowed. He left to draft the orders.

---

The decree reached the court the following morning.

Nam announced the findings in the flat voice that discouraged argument. Irregularities in the evidence. Minister Mok removed pending inquiry. The Westerner faction invited to resume participation.

Minister Yi stepped forward. "Prime Minister, surely the evidence of conspiracy—"

"Has been examined by His Majesty and found insufficient. The matter is concluded."

Minister Yi bowed and withdrew.

Sukjong watched the faces around the council table. The Southerners calculating. The returning Westerners calculating. The moderates calculating. No one satisfied. Everyone adjusting.

The stability achieved by the first purge had been destroyed by the attempt to extend it through fabrication.

---

Queen Inhyeon returned to the palace on a cold morning in late autumn.

Sukjong received her in the throne room. She performed the required obeisances, spoke the prescribed words of gratitude. Her voice was steady. Her hands were not. He noticed them shaking against the fabric of her ceremonial robe — a small tremor she controlled before the court could see it, the way a woman controls something she has been practicing on the road for days.

Five years had passed. The queen who returned was not the woman who had been sent away.

Lady Jang Hui-bin was not dismissed. She remained in the palace, her status reduced but not eliminated, her son still acknowledged as crown prince. The arrangement satisfied no one completely but provided everyone with reasons to accept what could not be changed.

Two queens occupied the same palace, neither comfortable, both aware that their positions depended on his ability to balance competing claims without resolving them permanently.

The restoration operated efficiently. The human consequences remained where five years of separation had left them.

---

In his private study that evening, Sukjong summoned Nam.

"The provinces," he said. "I need to know what survived."

Nam understood without elaboration. The purge had scattered the Westerner networks. The counter-purge had scattered the Southerner apparatus that had been hunting them. What remained in the provinces was an open question — and the Danjong restoration could not move forward without an answer.

"An inspection," Nam said. "Someone with rank enough to assess the ground without creating another cycle of fear."

"Open authority. Not covert. The provinces need to see the king's will, not suspect it."

"Inspector Yu. He has handled provincial assessments before. Steady, not brilliant. The local officials will not fear him and will not dismiss him."

"He may select his own team."

"He will. He keeps a small group. He will want one or two younger men for the road work — someone who can move through provinces without being recognized."

"That part of the assessment is the part I want."

"He understands."

Sukjong walked to the wooden chest and opened it. The formula was where he had left it, written in his own hand. For the first time since he had written it, the document was not an argument filed for the future. It was a plan waiting for the ground to be mapped.

He left the chest open.

"Send the order to Yu tonight."
