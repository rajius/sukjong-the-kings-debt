# Chapter 11 -- The Queen Restored

The evidence arrived before dawn on a gray morning in autumn.

Sukjong read the documents in his private study while Nam Guman waited in silence. The papers were convincing: correspondence between young Noron scholars, meeting schedules, fragments of plans to restore Queen Inhyeon through coordinated pressure on the throne. The kind of factional conspiracy that had justified the previous purge.

Except the handwriting was wrong.

"These letters," Sukjong said, setting down the evidence. "Who intercepted them?"

"Southerner agents. Minister Mok's network." Nam's expression was carefully neutral. "They claim to have discovered the plot through routine surveillance of Westerner activities."

Sukjong studied the documents again. The language was too crude, the political arguments too obvious. Real conspirators did not write letters that read like accusations against themselves.

"This is fabrication."

"Yes, Majesty. The Southerners have manufactured evidence to justify another purge."

"What do they expect?"

"Orders to arrest the named conspirators. Executions or exile for the leaders." Nam paused. "They believe you will support the action because it removes opposition to your marriage choices."

"The young men they have accused?"

"Scholars. Minor officials. Guilty of holding opinions, not of plotting rebellion."

Sukjong walked to the window. Five years earlier, he had eliminated Westerner opposition to preserve royal prerogative. That had created the political vacuum that allowed the Southerners to attempt this fabrication.

"Prepare orders for Minister Mok's arrest," Sukjong said. "And summons for the Westerner leaders to return to court."

Nam was quiet for a moment. "Majesty, that will constitute a complete reversal of the political arrangement established five years ago."

"Yes."

"The Southerners will not accept removal quietly. And the Westerners who return will not be the same men who were purged. Song Si-yeol is dead. The networks have been broken. What returns will be fragments, not the coherent faction that was eliminated."

Sukjong understood. The restoration would not restore what had been destroyed. It would simply create new forms of division, new sources of instability, new reasons for the court to distrust royal judgment.

But the alternative was to permit fabricated evidence to determine policy. That would establish a precedent far more dangerous than political instability.

"Issue the orders."

---

Nam Guman had become Prime Minister in the months after the Southerner rise to power, appointed partly for his competence and partly for his ability to manage the consequences of rapid political change. Now he faced the task of managing another reversal without bloodshed.

"The decree has been issued," Nam announced to the assembled court, his voice carrying the flat authority that discouraged argument. "The investigation into seditious activities has revealed irregularities in the evidence presented. Minister Mok and his associates are removed from office pending inquiry. The Westerner faction is invited to resume participation in government."

The silence that followed was not acceptance but calculation. Sukjong watched the faces around the council table, reading the adjustments each man was making to this unexpected reversal of fortune.

The Southerners who had expected to consolidate power were now facing investigation. The Noron scholars who had expected arrest were instead witnessing their accusers' downfall. The Soron moderates who had hoped to avoid another purge were discovering that moderation offered no protection from political upheaval.

Minister Yi stepped forward, representing the Southerner position. "Prime Minister, surely the evidence of conspiracy—"

"Has been examined by His Majesty," Nam interrupted, "and found insufficient to warrant the actions recommended. The matter is concluded."

The phrase was diplomatic code for an order that would not be debated. Minister Yi bowed and withdrew, understanding that further argument would be interpreted as resistance to royal judgment.

But Sukjong could see that the matter was far from concluded. The Southerners would not simply accept demotion. The returning Westerners would seek revenge for their previous treatment. The court would now be divided not between two factions but among multiple groups with conflicting grievances and competing claims to royal favor.

The stability achieved by the first purge had been destroyed by the attempt to extend it through fabrication.

---

Queen Inhyeon returned to the palace on a cold morning in late autumn.

Sukjong received her in the throne room. She performed the required obeisances, spoke the prescribed words of gratitude. Her voice was steady. Her hands were not. He noticed them shaking against the fabric of her ceremonial robe — a small tremor she controlled before the court could see it, the way a woman controls something she has been practicing on the road for days.

Five years had passed. The queen who returned was not the woman who had been sent away.

Lady Jang Hui-bin was not dismissed. She remained in the palace, her status reduced but not eliminated, her son still acknowledged as crown prince. The arrangement satisfied no one completely but provided everyone with reasons to accept what could not be changed.

Sukjong understood that he had created a situation where two queens occupied the same palace, neither comfortable, both aware that their positions depended on his ability to balance competing claims without resolving them permanently.

The machinery of restoration operated efficiently. The human consequences remained where they had been left by five years of separation and uncertainty.

---

In his private study that evening, Sukjong summoned Nam.

"The provinces," he said. "I need to know what survived."

Nam understood without elaboration. The purge had scattered the Westerner networks. The counter-purge had scattered the Southerner apparatus that had been hunting them. What remained in the provinces was an open question — and the Danjong restoration could not move forward without an answer.

"An inspection," Nam said. "Someone with rank enough to assess the ground without creating another cycle of fear."

"Open authority. Not covert. The provinces need to see the king's will, not suspect it."

"Inspector Yu. He has handled provincial assessments before. Steady, not brilliant. The local officials will not fear him and will not dismiss him."

"He may select his own team."

"He will. He keeps a small group. He will want one or two younger men for the road work — someone who can move through provinces without being recognized."

"That part of the assessment is the part I want."

"He understands."

Sukjong walked to the wooden chest and opened it. The formula was where he had left it, written in his own hand. For the first time since he had written it, the document was not an argument filed for the future. It was a plan waiting for the ground to be mapped.

He left the chest open.

"Send the order to Yu tonight."
