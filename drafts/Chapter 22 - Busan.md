# Chapter 22 -- Busan

He heard about Soonyi's marriage from Park Myeong-su, who mentioned it the way people mention weather that has already passed.

"The merchant's daughter. Her father found a good arrangement — an assistant section chief, capital yangban, older but well-placed." Park paused. "You knew?"

Yi Hwon nodded.

He waited for something to shift. For the news to open a door — the southern road, the seowon, the woman who had told him to go home. Soonyi was married. The handkerchief had been answered. The obstacle Lady Yoon had named was gone.

But he had understood, standing in that corridor with the sun behind her, that the handkerchief was the easiest thing she could say. Not the truest. The age was still there. The danger was still there. The machinery was still running. And the thing she had not said — the thing he had heard in the way she chose her seat and spoke his name like a door being closed — had nothing to do with Soonyi.

He had nothing new to bring her.

The Busan assignment came through Inspector Yu's office in the spring. Provincial grain discrepancies — port records that did not match the capital's numbers, shipment manifests that balanced on paper and broke apart under inspection. The kind of work no one wanted because the port merchants had connections and the local officials had arrangements and the last inspector who had been sent to ask questions had been reassigned before he finished asking.

Yi Hwon requested it.

Park Myeong-su watched him pack.

"Busan," Park said. "In spring. When the grain ships come in and the port officials are least interested in being inspected."

"That is why it needs to be spring."

"And you volunteered."

Yi Hwon tied his bundle. The knot held.

"You have been volunteering for everything since autumn," Park said. "The border review. The tax audit in Gongju. Now Busan."

Yi Hwon did not answer.

"You are not being diligent," Park said. "You are being reckless. There is a difference."

"Noted."

Park stood in the doorway. Yi Hwon settled the bundle on his shoulder and walked past him.

---

Busan in spring smelled of salt and dried fish and money.

The port handled grain from the southern provinces — rice, barley, millet — routed through merchant warehouses before shipment to the capital. The system was simple: provincial harvests arrived by cart, were weighed and recorded at the port authority, and loaded onto ships bound for the capital granaries. The records at each stage were supposed to match.

They did not match.

Yi Hwon spent the first week in the port authority office, reading ledgers. He presented himself as a clerk from the capital auditing seasonal discrepancies — a cover dull enough to be ignored. The senior port official, a man named Magistrate Cho, assigned him a desk near the window and lost interest.

The ledgers told a clear story if you read them the way Lady Yoon had taught him to read records — not for what they said, but for what they avoided saying.

The incoming weights were accurate. The outgoing weights were lower. The difference — roughly one part in eight — disappeared between the warehouse and the dock. Across a full season's grain shipments, the missing portion was worth more than the port authority's annual operating budget.

Someone was skimming a eighth of the grain and selling it through a separate channel. The port records had been written to absorb the loss. The incoming ledgers were honest. The outgoing ledgers were adjusted. And the adjustment was so consistent, so cleanly maintained across three years of records, that it could not be the work of a single clerk or a careless official.

It was a system. It had been running longer than Yi Hwon had been an inspector.

---

He began with the warehouse.

The head warehouseman was a man named Guk who had held the position for eleven years and who treated Yi Hwon's questions the way he treated rain — as something that would stop if he waited long enough.

Yi Hwon did not stop.

He visited the warehouse daily. He counted sacks. He compared the sack counts to the incoming manifests and found that the numbers matched in the morning and did not match by evening. Somewhere between the morning count and the evening loading, sacks were being diverted.

On the fourth day, he arrived before dawn and watched from the alley behind the warehouse. At the fifth hour, a cart pulled through the rear gate — the gate that faced the fishing quarter, not the dock. Two men loaded fourteen sacks and drove the cart south along the coast road.

He followed.

The cart went to a private warehouse three li south of the port, owned by a merchant named Han. The sacks went inside. The cart came back empty.

---

He spent the next week mapping the operation.

Han's warehouse served as a redistribution point. Grain skimmed from the port authority was stored, repackaged, and sold to private buyers at market price — bypassing the capital's price controls entirely. The profit margin was enormous because the grain had already been purchased by the government at regulated rates. Han was selling the same grain twice.

The operation required cooperation at three levels: the warehouse staff, the port authority clerks, and at least one official senior enough to ensure the outgoing ledger adjustments were never questioned.

Magistrate Cho.

Yi Hwon sat in his rented room above a noodle shop near the fish market and wrote his findings in the cipher Inspector Yu's office used for sensitive correspondence. He sealed the report and gave it to a courier heading north.

Then he went back to the warehouse.

---

The warning came on the twelfth day.

He returned to his room after dark and found the door open. Nothing was taken. His travel bundle was where he had left it. His notes — the ones he kept in his bundle, not the ones he had already sent north — were untouched.

But someone had been inside. The bundle's position had shifted. The knot was tied differently — not wrong, just different. Someone had opened it, read what was inside, and retied it. Carefully enough to pass a casual inspection. Not carefully enough for a man who tied the same knot the same way every time because his mother had taught him.

He sat on the floor and looked at the bundle for a long time.

A man with something to return to would have left that night.

---

He did not leave.

He went to the warehouse the next morning. Guk was not there. A younger man — new, nervous — told him Guk had been called to Magistrate Cho's office. Yi Hwon walked to the port authority.

Magistrate Cho was polite. He offered tea. He explained that the audit had been very thorough and that the capital would be pleased with Yi Hwon's diligence. He suggested that the remaining discrepancies were clerical — seasonal adjustments, rounding errors, the ordinary imprecision of a busy port.

"The outgoing weights are consistently one-eighth below the incoming weights," Yi Hwon said.

"Grain loses moisture in storage," Cho said.

"Not one part in eight."

"Inspector —"

"The loss is consistent across three years. It does not vary by season, by moisture, or by grain type. Rice, barley, and millet all lose exactly the same proportion." Yi Hwon set down his tea. "That is not evaporation. That is arithmetic."

Cho's face did not change. His hands did. They moved to his lap, below the desk. A man putting his hands where they could not be read.

"I will include your explanation in my report," Yi Hwon said.

He walked out.

---

The knife came two nights later.

He was walking back to his room through the fish market — closed at that hour, the stalls shuttered, the street lit only by the lamp he carried. He heard the footstep a half-second before the blade.

His training saved him. Not the civil examination. Not the provincial record-keeping. The training his father had given him before he chose the brush over the bow — the hours of reaction drills, the body memory of a boy who had learned to read an attack before he learned to read a text.

He turned into the strike instead of away from it. The blade caught his thigh instead of his side. Deep. He felt the heat before the pain.

There were two of them. The first had the knife. The second was behind him, cutting off the alley.

Yi Hwon dropped the lamp. In the dark, the advantage shifted. They knew the market. He knew how to fight without seeing.

The first man came again. Yi Hwon caught his wrist, turned it, used the man's weight to drive him into the stall frame. Wood cracked. The knife clattered. The second man hesitated — the half-second of a man who had been hired to intimidate, not to kill.

Yi Hwon picked up the knife.

The second man ran.

The first man was on the ground, arm bent wrong, breathing hard. Yi Hwon stood over him in the dark. Blood was running down his leg, warm inside his boot.

"Who sent you?"

The man said nothing.

Yi Hwon pressed the blade against the man's throat. Not hard. Enough to feel.

"Merchant Han," the man said.

Yi Hwon dropped the knife and walked to the noodle shop. He knocked on the owner's door and asked for cloth and hot water. The owner looked at the blood and did not ask questions.

He stitched the wound himself, the way his father had taught him to stitch field injuries — rough, functional, the kind of work that left a scar.

---

The report reached Inspector Yu's office before Yi Hwon did.

He stayed in Busan three more days — long enough for the wound to close, long enough to file the formal complaint against Magistrate Cho, long enough to ensure the courier carrying his sealed findings was two days ahead of anyone who might want to stop him.

He left Busan on the northern road, walking slowly because his leg would not let him walk fast. The journey that should have taken five days took nine. The wound opened twice on the road. He rested at inns where no one asked questions and ate meals he could not taste.

The capital was ahead. The work he was supposed to be doing — the southern survey Inspector Yu had been planning for months, the preliminary assessment of burial sites connected to the Danjong restoration — was waiting. It would keep waiting. The leg would not carry him south again until autumn.

He carried the bundle on his shoulder. The knot was the one his mother had taught him. Inside the bundle, the handkerchief was where it always was.

He had not taken it out in months.

---

The investigation led to three arrests. Magistrate Cho was stripped of his post. Merchant Han's warehouse was seized. The warehouseman Guk testified in exchange for leniency and confirmed that the operation had been running for six years.

Inspector Yu noted the results in his files. The name of the field inspector was not attached to the public record — protocol required anonymity.

But someone in Inspector Yu's office mentioned the case to someone in the king's circle. A young inspector in Busan who had been warned, and stabbed, and had stayed. Who had filed the report before he filed the wound. Who had chosen the work over the exit.

The name reached Sukjong's desk.

It was not the first time.
