# Chapter 27 — The Safe House

The house was half a day south of the ridge, set back from the road behind a stand of persimmon trees. A farmhouse to anyone passing — low walls, thatched roof, a kitchen garden along the south side. Nothing that announced itself.

Ok-sun was waiting inside. She had water heated and cloth prepared. She looked at Yi Hwon's arm, then at Lady Yoon, then back at the arm.

"This needs cleaning," Ok-sun said.

"I know."

Ok-sun did not ask who he was. She heated more water and left the room.

---

Lady Yoon unwrapped the arm.

The blade had cut along the outer forearm — long, shallow at the edges, deeper in the center where the force had concentrated. The field wrapping from the trail had stopped the bleeding but the wound was dirty. Grit from the rock face. Pine resin from the scramble. The edges were inflamed.

She cleaned it with warm water and a cloth, working slowly. He sat on the floor with his arm extended across a low table, his sleeve pushed past the elbow, his robe loosened on the wounded side.

He did not flinch. She had expected that.

"This will scar," she said.

"I have others."

She looked at him. His voice was flat. Which told her it wasn't.

She wrapped the arm in fresh cloth, tighter than before. He drew a short breath when she tied the knot.

"I will check it again in the morning," she said.

---

They talked through the evening.

Not about the cave. Not about the three years. About the work — the thing that had always been real between them, the ground on which both of them could stand without the floor shifting.

She told him about the burial records. Where they were kept. What they contained. The stone rubbings her husband had made of memorial tablets that the official record claimed were destroyed. The correspondence tracing where the Danjong loyalists had actually been interred — information that contradicted the county records and could not be allowed to surface until the restoration was certain.

He told her what the king needed. The burial site at Yeongwol assessed. The records located and secured. The formal documentation that would let the court issue a restoration based on evidence, not just royal decree.

"You have what he needs," Yi Hwon said.

"I have what my husband left. I have maintained it. But I cannot deliver it to the capital myself. A widow carrying historical records into the court's hands — the censorate would intercept it before it reached the king."

"That is why they sent an amhaeng-eosa."

"That is why they sent you."

The sentence sat between them. Not just operational. Not just the work.

---

He slept in the outer room. She stayed in the study. Ok-sun took the kitchen.

In the night she heard him move — the restlessness of a man whose arm hurt worse than he admitted. She heard him settle, shift, settle again.

She did not go to him. She lay in the dark and listened to the sounds of a man trying to find a position that did not press the wound against the floor.

---

In the morning she checked the arm.

The inflammation had reduced. The wound was clean. She re-wrapped it — the same tight knot, the same efficiency. He sat still and watched her work.

His robe had opened further during the night. The loosened fabric had shifted off his left shoulder. She could see the line of his collarbone, the hollow of his throat, the beginning of his chest.

And below the robe's edge, along his left thigh — a scar.

Not the arm wound. Not the scratch on his neck. Something older. Healed white, the way deep cuts heal — a permanent ridge of tissue, long, following the line of the muscle. The kind of wound that came from a blade wielded with precision by someone who knew where to cut.

She stopped wrapping.

"What is that?"

He looked down. His hand moved to adjust the robe — the reflex of a man covering something he had forgotten was visible.

"Busan," he said.

She waited.

"A grain investigation. Port merchants. The magistrate was protecting them." He adjusted the robe. It did not quite cover the scar. "I got too close."

"When?"

He was quiet for a moment.

"Last year."

She did the arithmetic without wanting to. Two years after she had sent him home. One year after Soonyi — though she did not know about Soonyi yet, did not know the name had been answered, did not know the handkerchief had lost its owner. She knew only the number: last year. And the scar.

---

She finished wrapping the arm. She tied the knot. Her hands were steady. She stood and walked to the window.

The persimmon trees were heavy with fruit. The morning light came through the branches in the way morning light does when it has nothing to hide.

She had told him to go back. To the handkerchief. To the girl who cared for him. She had put the sun behind her so he could not read her face. She had calculated every element of the refusal — the words, the positioning, the exit. She had been right. She had been correct in every particular.

And somewhere in the years after she sent him home, he had gone to a port city and gotten himself stabbed because a man with nothing to return to does not leave when he should.

She looked at the scar. The robe had shifted again. The white ridge of tissue ran along his thigh — permanent, structural, the kind of mark that changed a man's gait and did not fade.

She had done that. Not the merchant. Not the magistrate. Not the blade. She had done it by sending him away with nowhere to go and no reason to be careful.

---

She turned from the window.

He was sitting on the floor, his arm freshly wrapped, his robe loosened. He shifted to adjust the robe over the scar — and then stopped. He left it where it was. The thigh visible. The ridge of healed tissue catching the morning light.

Whether this was simple explanation or something else, she could not tell. He had positioned himself so she could see it clearly. A man showing a wound to a woman who had asked about it — or a man showing a woman something and waiting to see if she would stay close enough to look.

She walked back to him. She knelt to check the arm wrapping. Professional distance. The appropriate posture.

She was close enough to see the scar in detail — the white ridge, the depth, the way it followed the muscle. Close enough that her sleeve brushed his thigh when she leaned to check the knot on his arm.

She stood. She stepped back. She walked to the desk where the burial records were spread from the previous evening's work.

"We should review the Yeongwol routes," she said. "If the trail is compromised, the delivery path needs to change."

She sat at the desk. She picked up a document. She read the first line three times without understanding it.

He adjusted his robe. He came to the desk and sat across from her. He did not mention the scar. He did not mention the moment she had knelt close and then left. He picked up a map and began tracing the southern routes with his finger, the way he always worked — patient, thorough, waiting.

They worked through the morning. She corrected two of his route assumptions. He found a connection in the records she had missed. The work was real. The work had always been real.

But she could not stay inside it.

She caught herself looking at his hands instead of the map. At the wrapping on his arm. At the place where his robe met his collarbone. She caught herself and returned to the document. She caught herself again.

By midday she had stopped catching herself.
