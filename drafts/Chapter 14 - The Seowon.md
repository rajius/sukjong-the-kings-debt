# Chapter 14 — The Seowon

He spent the time at an inn in the county seat, writing nothing, asking no questions, letting the inn keeper believe he was a merchant waiting on a delivery.

On the third morning, he went to the seowon outside town.

He had identified it the previous day. Master Pak's seowon, a small academy that had survived the recent disruptions through a combination of low political profile and competent administration. The kind of place where a widow might quietly manage household correspondence and no one in the county would think to ask what else passed through her hands.

He arrived in the clothes of a minor scholar — a credible cover for a man who would be remembered later as having visited only once. He spoke briefly with Master Pak about an entirely fictional matter concerning provincial record-keeping. Master Pak suggested, with the polite reflex of a man who had been trained to suggest it, that a household member might have practical insights about local documentation.

Lady Yoon was crossing the courtyard with her arms full of cloth.

She saw him before he saw her. He felt the weight of her attention before he turned. When he did turn, her face was perfectly composed. She set down the cloth and bowed to him as a stranger.

"Master Park," Master Pak said. "Lady Yoon manages our household correspondence. You may speak with her in the small study."

She led him through the back of the building without a word. She closed the door behind them and turned.

"What are you doing in my sanctuary?"

"Asking the question you would not let me ask in the room with the body."

"Which question?"

"What are you going to do with the names he sold."

She did not answer. She moved behind the small desk that occupied most of the room, putting its length between them. Her territory. Her terms.

"Why does it matter to you?"

"Because no report has gone forward from me. I have written nothing about you, your network, or what I saw two days ago. If I had meant to expose you, I would not have come through Master Pak."

"That tells me what you have not done. Not what you want."

"What I want is to know who else is asking questions about your contacts. And how far ahead of them you still are."

"You believe I am ahead?"

"I think you would not be standing here so calmly if you were not."

She studied him for a long moment.

"You give me too much credit."

"No. Only enough."

The room was quiet. He let the silence work for him the way she would have let it work for her.

"These people," she said finally. "They are not yours."

"No."

"But you know something."

"I know the pattern is wrong for an official inquiry. Too patient. Too repetitive. Someone is mapping routes, habits, intermediaries."

"And in exchange for what you know," she said, "what do you want from me?"

"What you know about the people they are hunting. And what your contacts told you about who paid them."

"And what else?"

He hesitated.

"There is something else. But it is not part of the trade."

She waited.

"How old are you?" she said.

The question came out before she had decided to ask it.

He told her.

She looked at him for a long time. Then she looked away.

He did not look away.

"The work is real. That part stands. We meet twice more — you tell me what your contact revealed, I tell you what I find. After that, officially, I have found nothing in this county worth pursuing."

She was quiet for a long moment.

"And if I refuse?"

"Then you refuse. The work remains. That part was always real."

She studied him for a long moment.

"Two more meetings," she said finally. "And the other matter remains my choice."

He nodded.

He bowed and prepared to leave. At the door he stopped.

"You remembered me before I said a word."

She did not answer.
