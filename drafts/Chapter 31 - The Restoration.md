# Chapter 31 -- The Choice

Yi Hwon came back in the first month of the new year.

He had been gone three weeks, verifying the burial sites documented in Yeong-hak's records. The work had taken him through four counties, matching stone rubbings to actual grave markers, confirming that what the documents claimed existed where they said it existed. His verification report was complete — sixteen pages of attestation that the evidence was accurate and could be trusted by the court.

Lady Yoon heard his footsteps on the east path after dark and opened the door before he knocked. He looked travel-worn but satisfied. The kind of satisfaction that came from work completed properly.

"The sites match," he said. "All of them. Your husband's documentation was precise."

The records could now be delivered under royal protection, with his verification standing in place of the compromising correspondence. But that delivery required a decision she had not yet made.

---

On the second night after his return, she made it.

She was sitting at the desk when he came to her quarters, the lamp burning low beside her. He moved quietly, but stopped when he saw her posture — formal, deliberate.

"You went to Busan," she said without turning. "You walked into a port city where you had no contacts, no protection, and people were trying to kill you. You did that because I told you the work mattered."

"Yes."

"On the road, when they found us in the cave, you understood immediately what exposure would cost. Not just to the mission. To the people who had been carrying this for eight years."

He sat down across from her. "Yes."

"And you have read enough of the records to know what my husband died trying to preserve. You understand why the network exists. Why the work cannot stop."

She turned to face him.

"I am giving you the records."

She stood and moved to the desk where Yeong-hak had kept his most dangerous documents. The compartment opened under her hands. The papers were there — stone rubbings, correspondence, burial site documentation. Eight years of careful preservation.

"You have proven yourself worthy of carrying them forward."

---

Three days later, she woke in the middle of the night.

She stared at the ceiling. Her stomach was tight — low, persistent, familiar in a way that made her careful about hope.

Yi Hwon woke, feeling her restlessness. "What is it?"

She frowned.

"Are you sick?"

She nodded slightly.

His hand found the spot and pressed gently. The warmth helped. "How long has this been happening?"

"A few days. It's been a long time since I felt like this."

"You had this before?"

She closed her eyes. "Yes. The morning I sent Yeong-hak to the door on his last work trip."

The room was quiet. She could hear the insects outside. The creak of the roof beam that shifted in the cold.

"The physician said I was pregnant."

Yi Hwon's hand went still.

"And then?"

"I lost it when they brought him back."

---

By morning, she knew. She had known once before.

The same discomfort. The same timing. Her body remembering what her mind had filed away. No physician needed - she had learned to read these signs eight years ago.

---

When Yi Hwon came to her quarters that night, she told him before he asked.

"I will need care for the next nine months."

He sat down carefully.

"We would have to marry."

"Yes. Quickly."

She looked at him. "I handed you the records three nights ago. This does not change that decision. If anything, it makes the timing more urgent."

"I am using the cover identity. Marriage under a false name..."

"Creates legal complications."

"Yes."

She had been thinking all day. About the network, which depended on her being invisible. About Scholar Pak, who would lose his household manager. About the child, who would need safety and a name.

"You need to write to your superior."

---

Yi Hwon wrote to Yu that night.

*The source has been secured. Records ready for delivery. Personal complications have developed requiring marriage to regularize the situation. The cover identity presents legal difficulties that will require resolution.*

*The source's cooperation was granted before these complications arose. Her assessment stands on professional grounds.*

*Request guidance on administrative matters. - Yi*

---

Yu read the letter at his desk in the capital. He read it twice.

In twenty years of managing provincial assignments, he had not received quite this combination of success and complication. The man had secured the most important intelligence cache in a generation and gotten himself personally entangled with the source. The professional judgment appeared sound. The personal judgment remained to be seen.

He called for paper and brush.

The first letter would go to the administrative clerk. A routine notation: Inspector Yi Hwon's provincial assignment required documentation under assumed identity. Marriage record to be transferred to service file for legitimacy. The clerk would ask no questions.

The second letter would go to Yi Hwon.

*Inspector Yi: Your assignment has concluded successfully. Arrangements will be made to regularize your administrative position within two weeks.*

*Complete the delivery. Protect the source. Your personal entanglement does not void your professional accomplishment, provided both are managed properly.*

*The king requires the records before spring. - Yu*

---

They held a small wedding the following week.

Scholar Pak received the news with resignation. He had lost a capable household manager to a marriage that raised questions he preferred not to examine. He offered congratulations and made arrangements for her departure from the seowon.

To the neighborhood, a capital man marrying a provincial widow was unremarkable. He presented himself under his cover name. The ceremony was brief. Ok-sun stood witness.

But Lady Yoon watched Scholar Pak's face during the ceremony and saw the cost. He would have to find someone else to manage the correspondence, the accounts, the delicate balance of keeping a seowon running without attracting unwanted attention. Her departure left him exposed in ways that would take months to repair.

The network, too, would need restructuring. The widow who could move invisibly through the women's channels was gone. What remained was a married woman whose husband would draw scrutiny to any household they occupied.

These costs were real. She had chosen to pay them.

---

After the ceremony, Yi Hwon wrote to his family.

The letter was brief. He had married a widow of good family in the provinces. His assignment would conclude within the month. He expected reassignment to the capital by spring.

He did not explain the haste. He did not mention the pregnancy. As long as Yu handled the legal corrections, his family's approval was a secondary consideration.

He sealed the letter and set it aside. The important correspondence was complete.

---

That night she lay in the dark, his hand on her stomach, and thought about what had changed and what had not.

She had chosen this man weeks ago, based on his competence and his understanding of the work. The pregnancy had made delay impossible. The marriage had created safety for the child and legal protection for them both.

But the decision to trust him with the records — that had been made independently, before her body forced the timeline. He had earned access to the intelligence. Marriage had solved everything else.

She placed her hand over his. The child between them — the one she had told him would live. The work her husband had started, she had carried, and he would now deliver to the capital.

The cause and the life, finally aligned under the same roof.
