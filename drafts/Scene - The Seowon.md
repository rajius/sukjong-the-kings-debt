# THE SEOWON

*Placement: 1695, two days after "The Dead Man." Yi Hwon appears at Lady Yoon's sanctuary, forcing her into negotiation on his terms.*

---

Two days later, Yi Hwon appeared at the seowon.

Lady Yoon heard his voice before she saw him - something about grain storage records, spoken to Master Cho with the easy authority of someone who belonged. She was crossing the courtyard with barely a thought, her mind on the afternoon's correspondence.

Then she saw him.

Her step faltered. She caught herself against the wooden pillar, heart suddenly racing.

He stood in the main hall's doorway, relaxed, gesturing at a scroll. As if he had not been locked in her arms two days ago. As if he had not found her kneeling beside a dead man. As if nothing had happened at all.

Master Cho was nodding, clearly taking him seriously. Whatever credentials he was using, they were good ones.

Yi Hwon glanced up and met her eyes across the courtyard.

Neither acknowledged the other. But she saw him register her presence, saw the slight shift in his posture that told her he had expected her to be here. Of course he had.

She forced herself to continue walking, to nod politely to the other scholars, to behave as though her world hadn't just shifted fundamentally.

Behind her, she heard him conclude his business with Master Cho. Heard footsteps crossing the courtyard in her direction.

"Lady Yoon."

She turned. He bowed appropriately for a stranger meeting a scholar's widow.

"Master...?"

"Park. I'm researching provincial record-keeping practices. Master Cho suggested you might have insights about local documentation methods."

His cover was flawless. His manner exactly right for the setting.

"I know very little about such things," she replied carefully.

"Nevertheless. Perhaps we could speak briefly?"

She could hardly refuse without seeming rude. "Of course."

---

She led him toward the small study behind the library.

For the first time in eight years of hidden operation, she felt exposed. He had walked into her sanctuary like he belonged there.

She closed the door behind them and turned to face him.

"Well, Master Park," she said quietly. "What exactly do you want to know about documentation methods?"

He glanced once toward the window, then back at her.

"What is your plan for the names he sold?"

The question was so exact that for a moment she said nothing.

"I don't know what you mean."

"The dead man knew three contacts," he said. "You told me there were others asking questions. If he sold names, what have you already begun to move?"

She studied his face. No triumph. No performance. Only attention.

"Why do you care?"

"Because no report has gone forward from me."

That stopped her.

He let the silence hold a moment before continuing.

"If I had meant to expose you, I would not have come through Master Cho."

She moved toward the desk, putting its length between them. "That tells me what you have not done. Not what you want."

"To know who else is asking questions," he said. "And how far ahead of them you still are."

"You believe I'm ahead?"

"I think you would not be standing here so calmly if you weren't."

She looked at him for a long moment. "You give me too much credit."

"No," he said. "Only enough."

The room went quiet again.

"These are not your people," she said at last.

"No."

"But you know something."

"I know the pattern is wrong for an official inquiry. Too much patience. Too much repetition. Someone is mapping routes, habits, intermediaries."

She felt the truth of that at once. It matched too closely what she had already begun to fear.

"And in exchange?" she asked.

"My questions stop in the wrong offices," he said. "What I saw two nights ago does not enter the record."

"Until when?"

"Until you give me reason to regret it."

She almost smiled, but did not.

"What makes you think I'll agree to anything?"

"Because you need to know who else is looking," he said. "And because I think you know I am not the most dangerous person asking."

That, at least, was true.

She was quiet for a long moment.

"One meeting," she said. "To exchange information. Nothing more."

"Where?"

"I'll find you."

He nodded once.

Then, as if the thought had only just occurred to him:

"You remembered me before I said a word."

She did not answer.

But something in her face changed, almost too small to read.

He let that be enough.

"When?" he asked.

"When I know what can still be saved."

He inclined his head. "Then I'll wait."

---

She waited a full minute before following him out. In the courtyard, she could see him speaking with Master Cho again, thanking him for his time, gathering his scrolls like any disappointed researcher.

Master Cho approached her as Yi Hwon left. "Unfortunate that you couldn't help him more. He seemed like a serious scholar."

"Yes," she said, watching Yi Hwon's retreating figure. "Very serious indeed."
