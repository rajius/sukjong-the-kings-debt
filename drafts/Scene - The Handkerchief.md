# THE HANDKERCHIEF

*Placement TBD — during the working relationship, after the "Difficult" scene. The moment Lady Yoon discovers she is not the only woman, and discovers she cares.*

---

He said the name the same evening she told him about her husband.

They had walked most of the afternoon in silence. Near dusk, he said: "There's someone. In the capital."

He did not look at her. His voice was easy, almost light — the voice of a man making a small offering to balance the one he had received.

"Soonyi," he said. "We grew up on the same street."

He said nothing else. Lady Yoon nodded. "Childhood friends."

"Something like that."

They walked on.

---

Three days later, she was tidying the room at the inn — he by the door, she by the window, a folding screen between them. His travel pack had shifted during the night. She gathered what had spilled — a brush case, a folded map, a small cloth bundle — and as she lifted the bundle, something slipped from its folds.

A handkerchief. Silk. Not expensive, but good — the kind a prosperous merchant's household would produce. Pale blue-grey. The stitching along the edge was careful, even, patient. Not professional. Personal.

At one corner, embroidered in thread only slightly darker than the silk: 순.

She folded it, placed it back, and returned everything to his pack exactly as it had been.

---

The inquiry went out that afternoon.

She wrote a letter to a contact in the capital — one of Master Kim's former students — asking after a merchant family in the Jongno district. She embedded the question inside other business, so light it barely registered: *I understand there is a well-established family of that name. Do they have daughters?*

She sent it. She hated that she sent it.

---

The answer came twelve days later.

The family was established, comfortable. Textile trade. The father was known for honest scales. One daughter. Her name was Soonyi. Twenty-three. Unmarried.

Lady Yoon read the letter once. She folded it and placed it in the compartment where she kept correspondence that required no response.

---

That evening, alone in the room, she sat with the thing she could not name.

The handkerchief was in his pack against the far wall. She had held it for three seconds. Pale blue-grey. Patient stitching. The small character at the corner, placed exactly where his hand would touch it.

Someone had made that for him. Someone twenty-three and unmarried who had watched him leave for the provinces with a piece of her patience folded into his things.

And he had kept it.

The pain was so foreign she almost mistook it for illness.
