# Chapter 28 — The Morning

The second night was worse than the first.

She lay in the study and listened to him breathe through the wall. She knew the rhythm now — the way it deepened when his arm found a position that did not press the wound, the way it caught when he shifted wrong.

The safe house was secure, but temporary. He could not return to the inn.

She lay in the dark and planned.

---

Ok-sun's brother arrived at first light, as arranged.

He moved with the careful efficiency of a man who had done this before.

"The seowon," Lady Yoon told him quietly. "The back path."

Yi Hwon gathered his few belongings. His arm was better — he moved it carefully but without the guarding posture of the day before. They walked in silence through the early morning cold, following paths that avoided the main roads.

At the seowon gate, Ok-sun's brother bowed briefly and departed. He would remember nothing of this morning.

---

Lady Yoon found Scholar Pak in his study and explained the situation. A visiting scholar had arrived that morning - the genealogy researcher from three years ago. His work on provincial family records required extended access to local archives. The inn had proved unsuitable for extended research stays.

Scholar Pak received Yi Hwon with the quiet courtesy of a man accustomed to hosting scholars.

"The genealogy researcher," he said, looking at Yi Hwon. "You were here three years ago. Family records from Andong."

"Yes, Master."

"And you require accommodation while continuing this work?"

Yi Hwon explained: Provincial family records scattered across multiple counties. Research that required extended access to local archives and seowon collections. The work could take several weeks. He kept his injured arm carefully positioned beneath his sleeve.

Scholar Pak listened without expression.

"The quarters adjacent to the household manager's rooms have been vacant since autumn," he said. "You may use them. The work will be conducted during appropriate hours. The household will operate normally."

"The fee for accommodation and meals," Yi Hwon began.

"Will be arranged with the household manager," Scholar Pak said, glancing at Lady Yoon. "She manages such matters."

---

The quarters were small but adequate. A room for sleeping, a smaller room for study, access to the courtyard through a door that opened onto the same path Lady Yoon used when she needed to move through the seowon without being observed.

She showed him the arrangement while the students were at their morning lessons.

"The quarters around mine are always empty," she said. "Former students' rooms that are no longer used. No one pays attention to sounds from this section after dark."

"For the work," she added. "The records need preparation."

"For the work," he agreed.

---

He always sat nearest the door. She did not ask why. After the second evening she stopped noticing it as a choice and began noticing it as absence — the nights he was late, the empty chair on the wrong side of the desk.

The work filled the evenings. He brought a second lamp from his quarters. He rebuilt the fire without being asked. Small things. The quiet competence of a man who had lived alone long enough to manage a room without announcing it. She had lived with a man who forgot rooms existed. This was different.

One evening the work stalled. A route she could not verify, a name that would not resolve. Lady Yoon set the document aside.

"Why did you choose the civil service?" she said. "Your father was military."

Yi Hwon looked at her. Not at the question — at the fact of it. "You sent a letter. To find out about me." He paused. "Is that when you found the handkerchief?"

Lady Yoon kept her face very still. She could feel the warmth climbing her neck and willed it to stop at her collar.

Yi Hwon set down his brush. If he noticed, he did not say so.

"I did not want to go to war," he said. "I never saw the point. But the training my father insisted on turned out to be useful after I passed the munkwa. The road work requires a man who can sleep on the ground and move without being heard." He paused. "My father does not know this is what his training produced. He believes I sit at a desk."

"You fooled him?"

"Regularly. When I was fourteen he sent me to a garrison for summer discipline. I spent the month reading in the quartermaster's supply room. The quartermaster owed my father a debt and said nothing."

Lady Yoon started to smile before the story was finished.

Somewhere in the middle of the week, she stopped pouring one cup of tea.

Ok-sun observed the arrangement without comment. She brought meals to both quarters, swept both courtyards, and said nothing about Yi Hwon's evening lamp never being lit.

Scholar Pak asked no questions about the genealogy researcher's progress. The accommodation fees were paid promptly. The household operated smoothly.

---

On the seventh night, she heard him crossing the courtyard before he knocked. She had been listening for it.

The work was nearly complete.

They sat at Lady Yoon's desk, reviewing the final routes. The burial records were organized, the contacts verified. Yi Hwon would leave for the capital within days to deliver what Yeong-hak had died trying to preserve.

"The southern route is closed," she said. "We'll move the records through Jecheon instead. There is a contact there I trust."

He studied the map she had marked. He asked two questions about the Jecheon contact — good questions, the kind that showed he understood how networks failed and where trust was thinnest.

Then he reached across the table for the map she was holding, and his fingers touched hers.

Not deliberately. His hand arriving at the same corner of the map at the same moment hers was there.

She did not pull her hand away.

His fingers rested against hers. The map lay between them.

He looked at her hand. Then at her.

"Lady Yoon," he said quietly.

She looked at him.

"We should finish the routes," she said.

---

They did not finish the routes.

He withdrew his hand first. Not quickly. He picked up the map, rolled it, and set it with the others.

"The arm," she said. "Let me check the wound."

He extended his arm across the table. She unwrapped it. The wound was healing. The inflammation was gone. She cleaned it with fresh water. She rewrapped it. The knot was the same tight knot she always tied.

When she finished, she did not release his arm.

She held it. The wrapping between her hands and his skin.

She looked at him. He looked at her.

Neither of them spoke. Neither of them moved.

Then she released the arm and stood.

"Stay," she said.

She closed the door.
