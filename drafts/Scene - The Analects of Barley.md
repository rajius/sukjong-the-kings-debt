# THE ANALECTS OF BARLEY

*Placement TBD — during husband's temporary provincial posting (~1689-90, after Song Si-yeol's execution, waiting for permanent replacement). Pairs with the other two marriage scenes. Shows: the sparring, the wit, the husband's moral position on compromise vs principle.*

*Note: Original concept had Master Kim executed. Current timeline: Song Si-yeol is the one executed (1689). Master Kim still alive. Line should reference Song Si-yeol, not Master Kim's death. Adjust "Teacher Kim was executed" to reference Song Si-yeol.*

---

The magistrate had sent a gift. This happened occasionally — minor tokens to minor officials, the social grease that kept provincial government from seizing entirely. Usually it was dried fish or chestnuts. Today it was barley, two bags of it, delivered by a servant who bowed exactly the right amount and left without being offered tea.

Lady Yoon inspected the bags. The barley was good — better than what they bought at market. She mentioned this during the evening report, between the cloth merchant's wife's ongoing drainage dispute and the news that the schoolteacher had stopped drinking.

"The magistrate sent barley," she said.

"I know."

"It's good barley."

"Is it."

"Better than ours. I think it's from Andong."

He said nothing. He was reading.

"Andong barley costs twice what we pay," she continued. "Which means either the magistrate is being generous, or he wants something, or both, which in my experience is usually the same thing."

"Confucius says the gentleman accepts a gift without inquiring into its motive."

"Confucius never received barley from Magistrate Bae."

He turned a page. She thought the conversation was over. Then, without looking up:

"Mencius says if a man offers you grain unsolicited, examine whether he plans to ask you for rice."

She paused. "Mencius does not say that."

"He should have."

Lady Yoon looked at her husband. His face was expressionless, his eyes still fixed on the document. But the corner of his mouth had moved — a fraction, barely visible, the equivalent of another man's roaring laughter.

She returned to sorting the barley. "So what does the magistrate want?"

"He wants me to attend the autumn inspection at the granary and certify that the count is accurate."

"Is it accurate?"

"It will be after I certify it."

"Ah." She set down a handful of grain. "And is that what the barley is for? The certification?"

"The barley is for the barley. The certification is for the magistrate's career. The two are unrelated."

"Of course."

"Confucius says —"

"If you cite Confucius one more time about barley, I will serve it to you every meal for a month."

"Confucius also says the wise man knows when to stop speaking."

"That one he actually said."

"He was a wise man."

---

They ate the barley that week. She cooked it with jujube and pine nuts, the Andong way, because if a magistrate was going to bribe her husband with grain she saw no reason not to prepare it properly.

He noticed. He did not comment on the jujube. He did not comment on the pine nuts. He ate two bowls, which was one more than usual, and that was his comment.

Over the second bowl, she said: "Will you certify the granary count?"

"Yes."

"Even if it's wrong?"

He set his chopsticks down — not in the sharp way that meant she had overstepped, but in the deliberate way that meant he was choosing his words.

"The count will be close enough. Magistrate Bae skims, but he skims within reason. If I refuse, he'll find someone who won't notice the difference. If I certify, I know the margin, and I can make sure it stays within what the district can absorb."

"So you're protecting the district by helping him steal from it."

"I'm protecting the district by making sure he steals only a little."

She thought about this. It was, in its way, the most honest explanation of provincial governance she had ever heard.

"Song Si-yeol would have refused."

"Song Si-yeol is dead." Not cruelly. Factually. The way one names the cost of not compromising.

The room was quiet.

"The barley is good," he said.

"It's from Andong."

"I know." He picked up his chopsticks. "Confucius says —"

"Don't."

He ate. She thought she saw the mouth move again. She couldn't be certain. She never could.
