# THE EXAMINATION RECORD

*Placement TBD — after "Difficult" and "The Handkerchief." Yi Hwon discovers who the husband actually was. The moment he recalibrates everything.*

---

Yi Hwon told the archive clerk he was reviewing historical examination submissions. The clerk lost interest before the sentence was finished.

He was not studying prose.

Something about Lady Yoon's husband had never fit its frame. The intelligence she carried — the speed with which she read rooms, the capital-grade analysis she applied to provincial information — none of this was learned in a country seowon. She had been trained somewhere sharper. Which meant he had been somewhere sharper.

Yi Hwon pulled the munkwa records.

---

He found him in the second volume. First-attempt pass.

Yi Hwon's hand stopped on the page.

He had sat for the munkwa himself. He knew what a first-attempt pass meant. Over the entire dynasty, only about fifteen thousand had ever cleared the examination. Thirty-three slots every three years. The average successful candidate was in his mid-thirties, having prepared for decades.

This man had taken one of those slots on his first attempt.

He read the submission.

---

The essay was on governance — the obligations of rulers during periods of institutional strain. A standard topic. Most candidates wrote carefully. They balanced. They hedged. They demonstrated range without committing to positions that might offend examiners whose factional loyalties were known.

Lady Yoon's husband had not done this.

His essay was precise. Shorter than most in the volume, but every sentence carried weight. He argued that governance failed not when rulers were weak but when institutional structures permitted rulers to avoid accountability — and that the remedy was not better rulers but better structures of review. He cited the Spring and Autumn Annals with the kind of close reading that suggested he had not merely memorized them but had argued with them and won.

No hedging. No diplomatic ambiguity. He had written exactly what he believed and submitted it to a panel of examiners who could have ended his career before it started.

They didn't. They passed him.

---

Yi Hwon closed the volume.

Lady Yoon had not fallen from a provincial life to a provincial life. She had fallen from the capital. She had been the wife of one of the finest minds to pass through the examination system in his generation — a man who studied under Song Si-yeol, moved in the highest circles. The tea gatherings. The officials' wives. The factional map redrawn at every seasonal dinner. She had learned to read rooms not in a quiet countryside seowon but in the most dangerous drawing rooms in Joseon.

Then Song Si-yeol was executed.

Yi Hwon sat with the examination record open and saw what no list needed to tell him. A first-attempt munkwa pass. Song Si-yeol's student. Governance essays that argued for structural accountability with a clarity that left no room for misreading. After the execution, a mind like this — connected to the teacher, published, precise, refusing to hedge — would have been on a list immediately. Not eventually. Immediately. The apparatus did not wait. It did not need to investigate. The examination record itself was the evidence. Everything after Song Si-yeol's death was process.

The husband had been targeted long before he was killed.

She had retreated to the provinces. Back to Master Kim. And she had taken everything she learned in the capital and applied it to a scattered network of provincial contacts that no one would ever think to watch.

Yi Hwon walked out into the daylight.

*Difficult.*

*But he let me be myself the whole time.*

The handkerchief in his pack — pale blue-grey silk, patient stitching — felt suddenly like what it was. A thing from a simpler world.
