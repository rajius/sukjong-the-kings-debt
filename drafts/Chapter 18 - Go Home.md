# Chapter 17 -- Go Home

The days between the mill and the next meeting were the longest she had spent alone since the first year of the network.

She did not write letters. She did not receive contacts. She told Ok-sun she was unwell and would resume correspondence in a week. Ok-sun did not ask what kind of unwell. Ok-sun had been watching for two months and had already decided what the answer was.

Lady Yoon sat in the study with the door closed and tried to identify what was happening to her.

She was practiced at this. She had spent years learning to read rooms, to read faces, to read the gaps between what was said and what was meant. She had never had difficulty reading herself. Her own motives had always been legible — grief, anger, the focused patience that had driven the investigation. Clean lines. She had understood them the way she understood the network: as machinery that could be examined, adjusted, and maintained.

This was not legible.

She kept returning to the mill. Not to the intelligence exchange — that was clean, professional, the kind of work she could evaluate and file. She kept returning to the moment after the exchange, when he had asked "and then?" and she had looked at the mill wheel instead of at him.

She had looked at the mill wheel because looking at him had become difficult.

---

She thought about Yeong-hak.

Not the way she usually thought about him — the work, the network, the precision of his mind, the five years of Tuesday. She thought about the specific quality of contentment she had carried during the marriage. The attachment that had grown without either of them planning it. The evenings. The barley. The way he had never demanded anything from her and she had never needed to demand anything from him.

It had been enough. She had said that to herself many times in the years since his death. It had been enough.

She understood now what she had not understood before: that "enough" was not the same as "everything." The marriage had been real. The partnership had been genuine. She had been content. But there had been a register she had never entered — not because Yeong-hak had kept her out, but because neither of them had found the door.

The door was not a metaphor she would have chosen. But it was what she had.

---

She thought about Yi Hwon.

Not the way she had been thinking about him — as a problem to manage, a risk to contain, a complication in the network's operation. She thought about his hands. The way they looked when he crouched by the dead clerk's body — careful, still, reading the scene the way she read rooms. The way they had looked at the stream when he refilled the gourd. The way they had held his end of the mill conversation without pushing past the limit she had set.

She thought about the hold. The moment his body had changed under her arms — the tension recalibrating from escape to something else. She had stepped back because stepping back was what she did. She had been stepping back from things her entire life. The books in the seowon, the proposals she had refused, the questions she had not asked Yeong-hak, the answers she had not given herself.

She was thirty-five years old. She had not been touched by another person in five years. The hold in the dead man's room had been the first time in five years that her body had been close enough to another body to feel its warmth.

She had stepped back.

She sat with this for three days.

---

On the fourth day, she made her decision.

She chose the morning. Evenings were dangerous — low light, long silences, the kind of warmth that made careful people careless. Important things were said when the mind was cold and the light was honest.

He was packing his travel bundle by the door of the inn. She sat on the raised wooden floor of the corridor, her feet just above the courtyard stones. The morning sun was behind her, which meant he could not read her face clearly, which meant she had chosen where to sit.

"Yi Hwon."

He looked up. She almost never used his name.

"When this is finished," she said, "go home."

"I'll have to report to the capital first —"

"I don't mean the capital."

The courtyard was quiet. A rooster. The innkeeper's wife sweeping.

"The girl who made the handkerchief," she said. "Go back to her."

He set the bundle down.

"Lady Yoon —"

"You carry something from someone who cares for you. You carry it against your body." She paused. "I will not be the reason you set that aside."

"What if I don't want —"

"That," she said, "is exactly why you need to."

She stood. She picked up her travel bundle and settled it on her shoulder.

"The road to Yeongwol crosses the ridge at Baegun Pass," she said. "We should reach it before the midday heat."

She walked to the end of the corridor and turned the corner.

---

That night, at the waystation before the pass, she reported the day's observations as though nothing had happened. Road conditions. The state of the bridge. A rumor about the Yeongwol magistrate's deputy.

He listened. He took notes.

When she handed him the water gourd at the end of the evening, her fingers stayed a moment longer than they needed to.
